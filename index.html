The honest version

You can't 3× your speed and keep full comprehension. But you can get meaningfully faster.

Reading speed is limited by how fast your brain recognizes words and assembles meaning — not by your eyes. So we train the few levers research actually supports, and we measure comprehension every time so you never fool yourself.

RSVP reader

Words flash one-by-one at a fixed point so your eyes never move. Comprehension holds up to ~350 wpm; push past that only in short sprints. The amber letter is the optimal recognition point your eye should anchor on.

Ready

Guided pacer

A moving highlight pulls your eyes forward at a set pace, the way a finger or pen does. This is the single most evidence-friendly drill: it cuts regressions — the involuntary backward jumps that eat ~15% of reading time.

Chunk & peripheral-span trainer

Flashes a phrase, then hides it. Train yourself to take in 2–4 words per fixation instead of one. Fewer fixations per line = fewer stops = faster reading, and it naturally crowds out word-by-word subvocalizing.

Press start

Goal: read the whole chunk in a single glance without moving your eyes across it. If you can't, slow the flash down. Comfort first, speed second.

Eye-span grid (Schulte table)

Fix your gaze on the centre square, then find the numbers 1 → 25 in order using only your peripheral vision. A classic drill for widening the visual span you can take in per fixation. Beat your own time.

The science

What the research really says

Short, honest lessons. Where a claim is contested, we say so — and cite the source.

Why your eyes aren't the bottleneck

When you read, your eyes don't glide — they jump (saccades) and pause (fixations). But eye movements account for no more than ~10% of total reading time. The real work is lexical access: recognizing each word and integrating it into a sentence's meaning.

✺ Implication:Training your eyes to move faster has a small ceiling. Training comprehension, vocabulary, and efficient pacing is where the gains live.
Rayner, Schotter, Masson, Potter & Treiman (2016), Psychological Science in the Public Interest.
The speed–comprehension trade-off is real
✗ Myth:"Read 1,000–2,000 wpm with full comprehension."
✓ Fact:Beyond a point, going faster must sacrifice comprehension — you're sampling words, not reading them. That's skimming, which is a useful, separate skill.

RSVP studies show comprehension holding steady up to ~350 wpm, then dropping off as speed climbs. So aim to read your normal material in the 300–450 range with comprehension intact, and use deliberate skimming when you only need the gist.

Nocera et al. (2018), RSVP & inferential comprehension; Rayner et al. (2016).
Subvocalization: don't try to kill it

Subvocalization is the inner voice that "says" words as you read. Popular advice says eliminate it to break past ~200 wpm. The nuance: the inner voice supports comprehension, especially for difficult text, and fully suppressing it can hurt understanding.

✓ Better goal:Reduce full subvocalization of every word by reading in chunks and pacing slightly faster than comfortable. The voice fades to a lighter background trace on its own — you don't force it off.
Regressions: the quiet speed tax

Regressions are the small backward eye-jumps to re-read something — about 10–15% of saccades for typical readers. Many are unconscious and unnecessary. A pacer (finger, pen, or our Guided Pacer) gently discourages them.

⚠ Caveat:Some regressions are genuinely needed for hard text. The win is removing the habitual ones, not banning rereading entirely — forcibly preventing rereading lowers comprehension.
The thing that beats every trick: read more

The most reliable predictor of reading speed is practice and vocabulary. Familiar words are recognized almost instantly; a broad vocabulary and wide background knowledge mean fewer slow, effortful look-ups mid-sentence.

15 minutes of daily reading in a domain you care about will outpace any app drill over a month. The tools here accelerate that habit — they don't replace it.
